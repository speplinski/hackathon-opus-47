# Auditable Design — Hackathon Pitch

## 60-second pitch

Product teams do not lack feedback.

They have app reviews, support tickets, NPS comments, community threads, analytics, Slack messages — more signal than they can reasonably process.

But when that feedback becomes a roadmap decision, something critical disappears: the chain of reasoning.

A complaint becomes a theme.  
A theme becomes a priority.  
A priority becomes a backlog item.  
And six weeks later, nobody can clearly defend why that decision was made.

I built **Auditable Design** to preserve that chain.

It is a product-integrated research-and-validation agent that turns raw feedback into an evidence-backed design direction brief.

Not a summary.  
Not a generated redesign.  
A traceable argument.

The pipeline reads user feedback, clusters recurring friction, grounds it in the actual product surface, audits the pain through six design lenses, prioritizes the problem, proposes a direction, and then re-audits that direction before handing it to a human designer.

Every claim carries a path back to the original complaint and product evidence.

The pilot case is Duolingo’s new Energy system: users complain that a lesson is blocked mid-session. The agent maps that to the actual modal — “You ran out of energy” — where the user must buy Super, recharge with gems, or lose progress. That is no longer just sentiment. It becomes an auditable product decision.

Generic LLMs summarize feedback.  
Research platforms organize studies.  
Analytics tools show behavior.

Auditable Design creates the missing layer: **a defensible argument for what should change next.**

It does not replace designers or researchers. It gives them something much more useful than a summary: a direction they can inspect, challenge, reject, or build from.

That is the goal: not AI making design decisions, but AI making design reasoning auditable.

## 3-minute pitch

Most product teams are drowning in feedback.

They collect app reviews, support tickets, NPS comments, user interviews, community threads, analytics events, Slack messages. The raw signal is everywhere.

But the moment that signal enters the product process, it gets compressed.

A thousand complaints become five themes.  
Five themes become a roadmap discussion.  
The roadmap discussion becomes a backlog item.  
And then the actual reasoning disappears.

What exactly did users claim?  
Which product surface caused the pain?  
Which design principle was violated?  
Why was this problem prioritized over another one?  
Why is this proposed direction better?

Most teams cannot answer that clearly.

They can summarize feedback. They can tag comments. They can make a slide. But they usually cannot reconstruct the full argument from user voice to design decision.

That is the gap I built **Auditable Design** to solve.

Auditable Design is a decision layer between raw user feedback and the product backlog.

It reads large feedback corpora, identifies recurring friction, grounds those complaints in the actual product, audits the pain through named design heuristics, ranks the pain spaces, proposes a design direction, and then re-audits that direction before handing it to a human team.

The output is not a generated UI.  
It is not a magic product manager.  
It is not a replacement for research.

The output is an **evidence-backed direction brief**: a structured artifact that says what hurts, where it hurts, why it matters, what direction could reduce it, and exactly which evidence supports every claim.

The pilot cluster is based on Duolingo’s new Energy system.

Users complained that lesson completion was blocked by a paywall-like moment. The system mapped those complaints to the actual product surface: an in-lesson modal saying **“You ran out of energy!”** while the lesson is still active. The user is offered Super, a gem-based recharge, or an action that loses progress or XP.

That matters because this is not just “users are unhappy.” It is a specific interaction pattern: mid-session interruption, sunk-cost pressure, unclear cost model, monetization at the point of task completion, and possible streak anxiety.

Auditable Design turns that into a traceable structure.

It audits the interaction through multiple lenses: Norman for usability fundamentals, WCAG for accessibility, Kahneman for decision pressure, Osterwalder for business alignment, Cooper for interaction design, and Garrett for UX architecture.

Then it reconciles the findings, prioritizes the pain, proposes a direction, and verifies whether the proposed after-state reduces the same measured pain.

In the hackathon build, this pipeline is already operational across the research-and-validation backbone: ingestion, clustering, multi-lens audit, reconciliation, prioritization, direction generation, and iterative verifier-gated refinement.

The important part is not the severity score itself. The important part is the audit trail: every node, every claim, every rejected iteration, every link back to the user complaint.

This is where Auditable Design differs from existing tools.

Generic LLMs produce fluent summaries, but summaries are not decisions.  
Research platforms help teams organize studies, but they do not create an auditable bridge from full feedback corpus to design direction.  
Analytics platforms show what users did, but not why the design is painful in terms a designer can act on.

Auditable Design creates the missing artifact: a defensible argument for what should change next.

And the boundary is deliberate.

The agent does not ship.  
It does not merge.  
It does not replace the designer.  
It gives the designer a stronger starting point — one they can inspect, challenge, disagree with, or turn into actual design work.

My thesis is simple:

As AI enters product teams, the winning systems will not be the ones that merely generate more recommendations.

They will be the ones that make reasoning visible.

Auditable Design makes design reasoning auditable.

That is what I built this week.

## Slide / demo narrative

### Slide 1 — The problem

**Product teams do not lack feedback. They lack traceability.**

Feedback enters the system as evidence.  
It leaves the system as opinion.

### Slide 2 — The failure mode

A review says:  
“I was blocked mid-lesson and lost progress.”

A normal LLM says:  
“Users are frustrated with monetization.”

Auditable Design asks:  
Where did this happen?  
Which product surface caused it?  
Which design principles are involved?  
What direction would reduce the pain?  
Can we prove the reasoning chain?

### Slide 3 — The example

Duolingo Energy modal:

**“You ran out of energy!”**

Choices:

- Try Super / Get Super
- Recharge with gems
- Quit / Lose XP

This is a perfect audit case because it combines user frustration, visible UI, behavioral pressure, business logic, and design trade-offs.

### Slide 4 — The pipeline

Raw feedback  
→ clusters  
→ product grounding  
→ multi-lens audit  
→ reconciliation  
→ priority  
→ direction generation  
→ re-audit  
→ evidence-backed brief

### Slide 5 — The output

Not a summary.  
Not a UI mockup.  
Not a shipped decision.

A direction brief with:

- named pain spaces
- supporting complaints
- product evidence
- heuristic scores
- priority reasoning
- proposed direction
- rejected iterations
- confidence and limits

### Slide 6 — The category

Research tools organize evidence.  
Analytics tools measure behavior.  
LLMs summarize text.

Auditable Design creates the missing layer:

**an auditable argument for what should change next.**

### Slide 7 — The thesis

AI should not make design decisions for teams.

It should make the reasoning behind design decisions visible, testable, and challengeable.

## Strong closing

The point is not to automate taste.

The point is to make product reasoning inspectable.

If a team accepts the brief, they know why.  
If they reject it, they know exactly what they are rejecting.  
If they ship a different solution, the decision is still traceable.

That is the kind of AI I think product teams actually need: not louder recommendations, but better accountability.

Auditable Design is my attempt to build that layer.
